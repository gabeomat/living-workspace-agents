# The Funnel Matrix — types, the awareness × temperature engine, and architectures

Read before every build. This is the strategy brain: **the funnel type sets the default architecture; the awareness × temperature position tunes it.** Architecture first, emails second — always.

## The engine: locate the reader before writing a word

Two axes, asked in plain language during the strategy interview:

### Axis 1 — Awareness stage (what they'd have to come to believe)

| Stage | They know… | The sequence must… |
|-------|-----------|--------------------|
| **Unaware** | Nothing's wrong (or they've normalized the pain) | Name the problem vividly before anything else — lead with story and symptom, not solution |
| **Problem-aware** | The pain, not that solutions exist | Make the cost of the problem real, then open the door: "this is solvable" |
| **Solution-aware** | Solutions exist, not yours | Differentiate the *mechanism* — why this way works when what they've tried didn't |
| **Product-aware** | Your thing exists; they're not convinced | Proof, specifics, objection-crushing, risk reversal — education is done, belief is the gap |
| **Most-aware** | They want it; they haven't acted | Get out of the way: the offer, the deadline, the one-line reason-why. Short sequence, direct |

The stage sets the **belief checkpoints** — the sequence of things the reader must come to believe, in order, before the CTA is a reasonable next step for them. Write the checkpoints into the strategy brief; each email serves one.

### Axis 2 — Temperature (what the relationship can carry)

| Temp | Relationship | Tuning |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Cold** | Just met you (new opt-in, ad traffic) | More emails before the first real ask; heavier story/value ratio; earn the next open before selling |
| **Warm** | Knows you (engaged subscriber, event attendee) | Ask sooner; reference the shared history ("you were at Thursday's session") |
| **Hot** | Acted recently (attended, applied, almost bought) | Get specific and direct fast; the biggest risk is wasting their moment on warm-up they don't need |

**The two axes are independent** — an event attendee can be hot AND merely problem-aware (they showed up, loved it, still doesn't grasp your mechanism). Temperature sets pace and CTA weight; awareness sets content. Confusing them produces the two classic funnel failures: pitching cold traffic like they're ready (they unsubscribe), and educating hot buyers like they're strangers (they cool off and drift).

## Funnel types → default architectures

Each type below is a preset: default length, arc, and email jobs (jobs defined in `references/email-craft.md`). Present the default, tune it with the engine + the user's context, get a yes, then draft.

### 1. Lead-magnet welcome (opt-in → nurture → offer)
*Typical: 5–7 emails over 7–10 days. Reader: usually cold, problem- or solution-aware.*
Deliver the goods immediately (and make it consumed, not just downloaded) → origin story ("one of us") → the mechanism shift (why what they've tried hasn't worked) → proof story → transition to the entry offer → direct ask → open door. The classic mistake this preset prevents: five "value" emails with no bridge to anything.

### 2. Event funnel (webinar / workshop / challenge) — two halves
**Show-up sequence** (registration → event): *3–5 emails.* Confirmation with one consumption task → why-this-matters story → logistics + calendar nudge → day-of "we start in an hour." Job: get them in the room — show-up rate moves revenue more than any post-event email.
**Post-event split** (branch by what they did — ask the user which branches they can segment):
- **Attended, didn't buy:** *4–6 emails.* Acknowledge honestly → crush the #1 objection heard at the event (ask the user what it was — or pull it from their sales-call playbook if the sibling copilot is installed) → proof story → honest deadline → last call → open door + downshift offer if one exists.
- **No-show:** *3–4 emails.* Replay with a real expiry → the one big insight they missed (give it, don't tease it) → bridge to the same offer path, one step behind the attendee sequence.
- **Bought:** route to Post-purchase (type 7).

### 3. Application / booking funnel (applied or booked → the call)
*Typical: 3–5 emails across the gap.* Confirm + set expectations → authority/fit story ("who this works for") → pre-call homework that raises show-up commitment → day-before touch. Plus a **no-book chase** (2–3 emails) for people who started but didn't finish. Job: arrive warm, arrive at all.

### 4. Launch / open-close cart
*Typical: 3–5 runway emails + 4–7 cart emails.* Runway shifts beliefs BEFORE anything is buyable (story-led, checkpoint per email). Cart: open with the full offer plainly → proof + objection emails through the middle → FAQ/logistics → close day (2–3: morning reason-why, afternoon proof, final hours). Deadline discipline: the close is real or this preset is refused — a launch sequence with a fake deadline burns the list for every future launch.

### 5. Evergreen sales sequence (behind a VSL, quiz, or always-on offer)
The launch arc without the calendar: belief checkpoints → offer → proof → objections → a *personal* deadline only if one truly exists (enrollment windows, cohort starts, price changes). No manufactured countdown.

### 6. Re-engagement / list revival
*Typical: 3–4 emails.* Honest re-open ("been a while — here's what's changed") → pure value, no ask → one direct question or micro-offer → the sunset email ("want to stay?"). Rule: re-permission before re-selling. A revived list is earned back, not ambushed. (List-hygiene honesty: recommend the user actually remove non-responders at the end — deliverability is a compliance-adjacent kindness.)

### 7. Post-purchase / ascension (buyer → success → next thing)
*Typical: 4–6 emails over the first weeks.* Welcome + first-win instructions (buyer's remorse dies when they USE the thing) → success check-in → story of someone one step ahead → the natural next offer, framed as the obvious continuation. Sold on their momentum, not on fear of missing out.

### 8. Custom — the engine without a preset
When nothing above fits (their business model is different — that's expected, everyone's funnel is a little theirs): run the engine raw. Ask what the reader just did, where they are on both axes, what the one conversion goal is. Derive the belief checkpoints, set length by temperature, assign one job per email from the jobs library. Confirm the architecture like any preset. The presets exist to save time, not to limit the machine.

## The strategy brief (written before drafting, saved with every sequence)

One page in `nurture/sequences/<funnel>-<date>/brief.md`: funnel type · offer + price (from profile) · reader position (awareness × temperature, and what they just did) · belief checkpoints in order · email-by-email plan (job, story used, CTA) · timing plan · deadline (real / none). **Iteration reads this brief — never re-interviews, never re-derives.** It's the contract that makes Mode 3 cheap and keeps a rebuilt email consistent with its sequence.
