# Daily Business Pulse

**Every morning your business talks to you first. One scheduled brief — your key numbers with trends, what came in overnight, today's calendar, your tasks read against your weekly goal, and the single highest-leverage move for the day — pinned in your sidebar before you've had coffee.**

Most owners start the day by *hunting*: open Stripe, open the email platform, open the calendar, open the task list, try to hold it all in your head, and still not know what actually matters today. The Pulse inverts that. The hunting is done before you wake up, and it arrives with a point of view — not a dashboard you have to interpret, a briefing that's already made the call.

## What happens when you run it

1. **First run only — it learns what "healthy" means for *your* business.** A quick interview: what 3–5 numbers tell you your business is healthy, and where does each live? A connector, an export you can drop in a folder, a file you already keep, or just your say-so — every metric has a working path, and it tests each one with a real read before trusting it. It also maps your task/goal file (creating a simple one if you have nothing), your calendar, your vocabulary, and your brand colors. Then it generates **Pulse #1 on the spot** and offers to schedule the daily run.
2. **Every morning: it gathers, remembers, and computes.** Each metric is pulled from its source, and one row is appended to your rolling **pulse log** — which is how trends exist even if you type your numbers in by hand. Deltas and sparklines come from that log, deterministically. It never invents a number, and every figure on the page carries its source and freshness ("stripe · live", "csv · Jul 8", "your last update · Jul 5").
3. **It names The One Thing.** Fusing your weekly goal, the freshest data, and your latest focus note, it puts a single move at the top of the page — with the why, and a first action you can take in 30 minutes. If your stated focus and the data disagree, it names the tension and makes the call. If today's right move is *"change nothing, let it run,"* it says that with the same confidence.
4. **You get a briefing, not a data dump.** Six sections in a clean editorial page: The One Thing → your numbers → what came in overnight → today at a glance → tasks & goals (including honest drift-spotting between your goal and your activity) → the ranked top 3, at least one protecting revenue and one growing it. Pinned as a living artifact in Cowork; a self-contained HTML file everywhere.
5. **It ends with one question: "What's your focus today?"** Your one-line answer gets logged — and tomorrow's One Thing is built on it. The pulse gets smarter the longer you talk to it.

## What you end up with

A two-minute morning read that replaces the app-hopping ritual, a pinned dashboard that's always today's, a growing trend history of the numbers you chose (built automatically, even from hand-typed values), and one clear call every day about where your attention goes.

## What you need

- **3–5 numbers you care about** and *any* way to get them — a connector (Stripe, your email platform, your community), an export you can download, a file you already keep, or just telling it. No connector is required; a pulse built entirely from asked numbers is a legitimate pulse.
- **Optional — a calendar connector** (Google Calendar) so "Today at a Glance" is automatic; without one you can paste your day or skip the section.
- **Optional — a task/goals file** — it reads yours (read-only, always) or creates a simple one with you.
- **Optional — a Business Brain / `CLAUDE.md`** so The One Thing understands your projects, not just your numbers.
- **Works in both Cowork and Claude Code** — living pinned artifact in Cowork, self-contained HTML everywhere.

## Honest limits — and this one matters

The Pulse is **read-only toward your files**: it reads your task list, your trackers, your connectors — it writes only its own briefing and its own log. It never edits your to-dos, never moves money, never sends anything. It also never fakes data: a broken source is reported as a broken source, a stale number is labeled with its date, and a metric you're not tracking yet says exactly that. Trends need history — sparklines start forming after about a week of runs. And it's an advisor at 7am, not an oracle: it makes the sharpest call it can from the data you gave it, and tells you when that data is thin.
