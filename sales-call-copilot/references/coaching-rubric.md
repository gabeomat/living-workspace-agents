# The Coaching Rubric — seven dimensions, scored with evidence

Read this before every debrief. The rubric is **fixed** — it is the coaching IP. What varies per user is their offer, their motion, and their growth edge (tracked across scorecards), never the dimensions themselves.

## How to score

- Each dimension gets **1–5** plus a one-line evidence citation from the transcript (quote or timestamp). A score without evidence is not a score.
- **3 is competent.** 5 is rare and must be earned. Don't inflate to be kind — kindness lives in the delivery, not the number.
- **N/A is honest** — a discovery call may never reach price; score what the call actually contained and say why the rest is N/A.
- End every scorecard with **The One Thing**: the single highest-leverage change for the next call. One. The other six dimensions wait their turn — a person can fix one habit at a time, and a scorecard that asks for seven changes produces zero.
- Praise with the same specificity as criticism. "At ~12:00 you let the silence sit after asking about budget — that's exactly right" teaches as much as any correction.

## The seven dimensions

### 1. Talk ratio
Who did most of the talking? On discovery-weighted calls the prospect should carry most of it (rough guide: prospect ≥ 60%). Estimate from the extraction — exact percentages aren't the point; "you talked for the first eleven minutes" is.

### 2. Discovery depth
Did the seller find the *real* problem, or accept the surface answer? Look for: the goal behind the stated goal · what the problem costs them (money, time, stress — in the prospect's words) · why now · who else decides · what they've already tried. Counting questions isn't the measure; **follow-up questions** are — "tell me more about that" beats five new topics.

### 3. Listening & objection handling
When the prospect pushed back or hesitated: was the objection heard fully before it was answered? Was it answered honestly — or dodged, talked over, or "handled" with pressure? Best pattern: acknowledge → clarify ("what's behind that?") → answer plainly → check ("does that land?"). An objection surfaced and *explored* is progress; an objection smothered comes back as a lost deal.

### 4. Price delivery
When money came up: was the price stated plainly and *then held* — or cushioned, rushed past, or negotiated against by the seller before the prospect even responded? The craft: **state the number, then hold the silence.** The first voice after the price should be the prospect's. Also score: was price anchored to the value discussed, or dropped in cold?

### 5. Confidence leaks (the four patterns)
The most common way good sellers quietly kill their own deals — apologizing for the sale. Scan explicitly for each; don't trust a "felt fine" gut check:

1. **Pre-emptive self-disqualification.** *"I don't even know if this is for you…"* / *"this might not be perfect, but…"* — talking the prospect out of it before they're in.
2. **Apologizing for standard sales mechanics.** Deadlines, prices, payment terms delivered with a wince: *"I'm sorry to make this so urgent…"* / *"I know it's a lot, but…"* — telling the buyer the mechanics can't be trusted.
3. **Self-deprecating the materials.** *"This slide isn't great…"* / *"I threw this together…"* — transferring the seller's doubt onto a buyer who was ready to trust the material.
4. **Hedging the offer itself.** *"I think it could maybe help…"* / *"if you want, you could possibly…"* — training the buyer to hedge on their own decision.

The default substitution, whenever one surfaces: **"Here's what I built and why."** / **"If you want help with this, here's how."** State it as fact; let the buyer decide.

**The critical nuance — sincerity is the asset; apologizing is the leak.** These are different things, and flattening both loses the seller's best quality. *Keep:* warmth, humility about a genuinely early product ("we're in the founding round — still refining" as description, not defense), acknowledging the buyer's agency, honest "why I built this" stories. *Strip:* the four patterns above. A confident close isn't pushy — showing someone value and then not offering a solid way to get help with it is an incomplete service. When coaching this dimension, never sand the human off; name the specific leak, keep the warmth around it.

### 6. The ask
Did the seller actually ask for the sale (or for the concrete next step the call was supposed to produce)? A call that ends in "well, think it over and let me know!" scores low even if everything before it was excellent — the close is part of the service, not an imposition on it. Score the directness and the timing: was the ask made when the buying signals appeared, or deferred past them?

### 7. Next-step clarity
Does the call end with a **specific, mutual, dated** commitment — "I'll send the proposal Thursday; we talk again Tuesday at 2" — or a fog ("I'll follow up soon")? Deals age to death in the fog. Also capture what the *seller* owes: those promises go straight to the pipeline, because a seller who misses their own commitment torches more trust than any objection.

## The trend layer (Mode 5)

Scorecards accumulate; that's where the real coaching lives. Across 3+ calls look for: a dimension consistently lowest (that's the growth edge — recommend The One Thing from there until it moves) · a leak pattern recurring in the same call phase · what scored differently on won vs. lost deals. The trajectory to aim for: first the copilot flags the habit → then the user catches it live before the debrief does → then it's gone. Say so when a user crosses one of those thresholds — measured progress is the most motivating coaching there is.
