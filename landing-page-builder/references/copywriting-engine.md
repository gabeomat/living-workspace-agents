# The Copywriting Engine — conversion craft, in the user's voice

A landing page lives or dies on its copy. A gorgeous page with weak words is a failed page. So the copy on every page this agent builds is **written to convert** using real direct-response craft and buyer psychology — *and* it sounds like the actual person whose business it is. Both, always. Never one at the expense of the other.

This is the FIXED IP (the persuasion structure and psychology) meeting the PULLED identity (the user's voice). The structure is the same for everyone; the voice is theirs.

---

## The voice rule (pull this before writing a word)

Find the user's voice in this order and defer to the first that fits:

1. **A brand-voice / voice-writing skill, if one is available in the environment.** If the user has a voice skill (e.g. a `*-voice-writer` or `brand-voice` skill), **route the copy through it.** The engine below decides *what the copy must do*; the voice skill decides *how it sounds*. This is the ideal case — great structure + their real voice.
2. **Voice inferred from the workspace / Brain.** No voice skill? Infer the voice from existing copy, brand docs, past posts, an About page, the profile. Match their rhythm, vocabulary, and point of view.
3. **The profile's voice notes,** if a saved profile names them.
4. **Ask briefly,** only if voice is genuinely unknowable from context.

Say which voice source you used. If none was available, write clean, human, persuasive copy and note that no specific voice source was found.

---

## Universal copy guardrails (apply to every page, every user)

These are not style preferences — they are conversion rules. They hold no matter whose page this is.

- **Persuasive, not hard-sell.** These pages convert through clarity, desire, and belief — not pressure, shouting, or manipulation. No fake countdown timers, no "ONLY 2 SPOTS LEFT" unless it's literally true. Manufactured scarcity reads as desperation and kills trust.
- **No apologetic selling.** Never soften the ask with hedges, over-explaining, or "I know this might not be for everyone, but…" energy. State the offer and the reason plainly: *here's what this is and why it's worth it.* Confidence converts; apology leaks.
- **Lead with the shift, not the pitch.** Open on the change/insight/tension the reader already feels, not "Introducing [Product]." Earn the pitch by naming their world first.
- **Specific beats clever.** A concrete promise ("a week of posts from one video") beats a clever abstraction. Cut adjectives that don't carry weight.
- **Human-work framing.** When the value is "replaces a vendor / saves labor cost," do NOT lead with "fire your [bookkeeper / VA / SEO person]." Keep the dollar impact but frame it as *more profit, run leaner, reclaim your time* — not headcount cuts. Only use replace-a-human framing if it's genuinely the strongest lever, and flag it rather than defaulting to it.
- **Honesty is a conversion asset.** No invented proof, no borrowed testimonials, no claims the business can't back. Real specificity outperforms inflated claims and survives scrutiny.
- **One promise, one action.** The page makes one core promise and asks for one thing. Every line either builds that promise or removes a reason to say no.

## The persuasion spine (adapt sections to the page's one goal)

Not every page uses every beat, and the order flexes to the concept — but a converting page moves the reader through this arc. Map the brief's ONE conversion goal onto it.

1. **Hook / above-the-fold** — the shift + who it's for + the core promise, in the first screen. The reader must know within seconds: *what is this, is it for me, why should I care.* Primary CTA visible here.
2. **The problem, felt** — name the reader's current pain/tension in their words (pull real buyer language from the Brain/profile where possible; don't paraphrase in seller-voice). Make them feel understood before you offer anything.
3. **The mechanism / the shift** — the new way of seeing it, the thing that makes the promise believable. This is where you earn the sale intellectually.
4. **The offer** — what they get, concretely. Outcomes first, features second. Make the transformation vivid.
5. **Proof** — real evidence: results, testimonials, credentials, demonstrations — only what's true and available. If proof is thin, lean on specificity and a demonstrable mechanism instead of inventing it.
6. **Objections dissolved** — answer the top 2–3 "yeah but…" reasons they'd hesitate (price, time, "will it work for me," risk). A short honest FAQ or a guarantee if one exists.
7. **The close + CTA** — restate the core promise, remove the last friction, make the one action obvious and low-effort. Repeat the primary CTA. No competing asks.

## Self-check on the copy (before render)

- [ ] Real, specific, final copy — no placeholder, no lorem.
- [ ] Sounds like the user (voice source named), not a template or another user.
- [ ] Persuasive through clarity/desire/belief — no hard-sell, no fake scarcity, no apologetic hedging.
- [ ] Leads with the shift; one promise; one action.
- [ ] Every claim is true and backable; human-work framing honored where relevant.
- [ ] The reader knows what/who/why in the first screen, and exactly what to do at the end.
